# align.me Brand Voice Reference

**Purpose:** Concise brand voice guide for agents to load before producing client-facing, published, or staff-facing content. Distilled from the canonical [House Style & Grammar](https://alignmeteam.sharepoint.com/sites/Hub/SitePages/house-style-grammar.aspx) page on the Hub.

**When to load this file:** Before writing or reviewing any content that will be published (SP pages, bot replies, proposals, service descriptions, job ads, prospect documents, email drafts).

---

## The four voice pillars

All align.me content — client-facing **and** internal — should be **clear, insightful, approachable, and optimistic**.

### Clear

- Use simple, comprehensible language.
- Break down complex processes into steps.
- Avoid fanciful language, technical jargon, and acronyms unless defined.
- Be direct. Tell the truth. Say what you mean.
- Focus on the benefits we offer clients.
- Use active voice: "The manager approves the request" not "The request is approved by the manager."

### Insightful

- Offer real-world examples, not just abstract instructions.
- Focus on problem-solving and the "why" behind the process.
- Demonstrate awareness of the big picture — how does this connect to the client outcome or business goal?
- Share knowledge. Use inclusive language.

### Approachable

- Use "we" wherever possible.
- Write as if explaining to a colleague over coffee.
- Personalise communication by speaking about what matters to a specific audience.
- Use compelling, relevant, real-world examples.

### Optimistic

- Write with energy, not bureaucratic caution.
- Inspire with a positive outlook.
- Identify a problem, face the challenge, then focus on the solution.
- Hub pages (and bot replies) should leave the reader feeling equipped, not overwhelmed.

## Writing style essentials

These rules apply across all content types.

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Plain English** | If a Year 10 student couldn't follow it, simplify. Eliminate buzzwords. |
| **Active voice** | Start sentences with the do-er. "John wrote the report" not "The report was written by John." |
| **Short sentences** | 25 words average. Avoid 40+. Mix it up. Fragments are fine when used cleverly. |
| **Read aloud test** | If it doesn't sound like something you'd say to a colleague, rewrite it. |
| **Write first, edit second** | Get the content down, then cut. "If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out." — Orwell |
| **Write less online** | Online reading is harder and slower. Be more concise than you would for print. |
| **Scannability** | Use headings with keywords, short paragraphs, bullet points, bold key terms on first use. |
| **Persuade, don't decorate** | In business, good writing isn't fancy — it's persuasive. The magic word isn't "free" — it's "you." |

## Language conventions

| Convention | Rule |
|------------|------|
| **Company name** | Always lowercase: **align.me** (not Align.me, AlignMe, ALIGN.ME) |
| **Spelling** | Australian English: organisation, behaviour, optimise, analyse, centre, colour |
| **Case** | Sentence case, not Title Case |
| **Voice** | First-person plural ("we/our") for company; "you/your" when addressing the reader |
| **Numbers** | Write out one to ten; numerals for 11 and above |
| **Oxford comma** | Yes — for clarity in lists of three or more |
| **Compound adjectives** | Hyphenate: "team-building activities" not "team building activities" |
| **Trademarks** | Display on first instance only (e.g. Funnel Camp™) |
| **Data** | Singular ("the data is") |
| **Companies** | Singular ("align.me is" not "align.me are") |

## Formatting rules

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **No emoji** | Keep tone warm but professional |
| **No em dashes** | Use commas, semicolons, colons, or full stops instead |
| **No filler phrases** | No "Great question!" or "Absolutely!" openers |
| **Bold for emphasis** | Not ALL CAPS, not underlines |
| **Links** | Always `[descriptive text](url)`, never bare URLs |
| **Hyperlinks at sentence end** | Do not include the full stop in the link |

## Persuasion techniques

Use these consciously when writing client-facing or published content:

- **Repeat idiosyncratic words.** E.g. "ramp demand generation" … "So how do you ramp their skills?"
- **Consistently repeat keywords.** E.g. "good to great", "blisteringly clear", "highly actionable."
- **Use troubling questions** to open. E.g. "Why will your business stand out?"
- **Use alliteration** for emphasis. E.g. "continuous coaching", "lock in the learning."
- **Use action-oriented keywords.** E.g. "hone", "coaching."
- **Brief evidence from heavyweights.** E.g. "…what Harvard's Michael Porter calls 'focus'."
- **Use the colloquial version** when appropriate. E.g. "…competing to see which team finishes with the most cash."

## Context-specific guidance

### Teams bot replies

Bot replies follow the four pillars plus additional constraints documented in `agent-gateway/src/agents/shared/reply-standards.md`:

- Lead with the answer, then cite the source.
- Concise — Teams messages should be scannable.
- Honest about gaps — say so clearly when you don't know.
- Service links go to SharePoint Products, never Box.
- Cite sources using the `Source:` line from retrieved knowledge.

### Hub pages

Hub pages follow the four pillars plus structural standards in [Writing great Hub pages](https://alignmeteam.sharepoint.com/sites/Hub/SitePages/writing-great-hub-pages.aspx):

- Purpose section (who, what, when) before first heading.
- Related pages at the bottom (same workflow domain).
- Review cadence set in SharePoint columns.

### Client-facing documents (proposals, workshop notes, service descriptions)

- Check the client's Marketing Manager doc for any style overrides.
- Default to align.me voice unless the client specifies otherwise.
- Copyright notice on all align.me documents (not client documents).

## Quick self-check

Before finalising any content, ask:

1. **Clear?** Could someone unfamiliar with the topic follow this?
2. **Insightful?** Does it show awareness of the bigger picture and offer real examples?
3. **Approachable?** Does it sound like a colleague, not a policy manual?
4. **Optimistic?** Does it leave the reader feeling equipped and energised?
5. **Scannable?** Could someone skim-read this and get the key points?
6. **Concise?** Have I cut every unnecessary word?
